Chapter 3
Deductive Reasoning

Mathematicians go about understanding the world in a very rigorous manner; they do not guess or believe things, but they prove them. Once a fact has been proven, it can be used for proving other facts. In this chapter, we take a close look at how to operate in this framework. As a human being, you are already adept at reasoning about the world, so some of what follows will likely be familiar to you already. Turning your intuitive understanding into explicit knowledge will improve your ability to reason this way.

3.1: Gathering Knowledge

Deductive reasoning is the process of concluding facts from assumptions. If we assume that squirrels are always hungry, and that Alex is a squirrel, we can conclude that Alex is always hungry. A framework for deductive reasoning must provide clear rules on which deduction steps are valid. If, for example, we knew that Vic was always hungry, we should not be able to conclude that Vic is a squirrel; Vic could be a badger for all we know.

Contrast this with inductive reasoning, the process of hypothesizing causes from observed facts. From observing that the sun rises and sets every day, you might form the potential explanation that the sun orbits the earth. Later observations might invalidate a hypothesis, but a hypothesis can never be proven beyond doubt.

Even though inductive reasoning can lead to false conclusions, it has its firm place in humanity's quest for knowledge. Physics or chemistry, for example, would make little sense if they were not grounded in observations of the world around us. They can strive to find increasingly accurate models of the world, but there can always be a new experiment that contradicts the existing models.

Unlike the natural sciences, mathematics relies on deductive reasoning alone. Rigorous adherence to the principle that every claim has to follow from either assumptions or previously proven claims may not be a normal mode of operation for your brain, but learning to operate that way comes with a number of benefits:

  • Proven knowledge is permanent. It does not degrade, it does not go out of style, it will not be falsified by a future experiment. This has enabled mathematicians to build up knowledge over centuries.
  • Proven knowledge is verifiable. It does not matter who wrote a proof, whether you trust them or not. If every single step follows the deduction rules, then the resulting information is true.
  • Proven knowledge is inspectable. If you do not understand why something is true, the proof breaks it down for you into small, almost obviously correct steps. More often than not, the person who wrote that initially incomprehensible proof is yourself from the past. But if the proof is well written, you are always able to untangle it.
  • Proven knowledge is composable. Once a fact has been proven, it can be reused in further proofs. Furthermore, the details of how it was proven do not affect that reuse in any way.

Once mastered, deductive reasoning enables you to think thoughts you would not have been able to think otherwise, because you would have been overwhelmed by the sheer amount of details and the accumulation of tiny errors.

A key property of deductive reasoning is that the mere form of a statement already implies how it can be proven. Suppose we want to prove that there is at least one hungry badger, how do we go about it? By presenting an actual hungry badger. How do you prove that there is at least one flying squirrel? By presenting an actual flying squirrel. If however we wanted to prove that all squirrels can fly, presenting an individual flying one would not help at all.

Consequently, the fewer different forms of statements there are, the less complicated the system of deductive reasoning becomes. In the next section, we examine a small number of constructs for forming statements, chosen so that any statement of interest can be expressed using these constructs. The accompanying examples may feel trivial or even boring in their simplicity. That is an incredibly important and desired aspect of the system. Proving facts should not in itself be difficult; all complexity should come from the subject matter at hand, not the proof mechanism itself. Humanity has expended a great deal of collective brainpower so that the contents of the following section would become as simple as possible.

3.2: A Whirlwind Tour of Tiny Proofs

In order to formulate statements, we first need objects that the statements can talk about. For the coming examples, we will primarily use the natural numbers (0,1,2,…0, 1, 2, \ldots0,1,2,…). To demonstrate that this mode of reasoning can be applied to anything really, we will also talk about a population of friendly (and hungry) squirrels: Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex, Blake\mathrm{Blake}Blake, and Charlie\mathrm{Charlie}Charlie.

A squirrel by itself is not a statement; Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is neither true nor false, but merely an adorable object of interest. The same applies to the number 171717, although arguably less adorable. We can bridge into the world of statements by comparing objects for equality:

Definition 3.1: Equality

Two objects are considered equal if they are identical: everything is equal to itself, but not equal to anything else. Claiming that two objects are equal is a statement. We write x=yx \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} yx=y to compactly express that the object xxx is equal to the object yyy.

Now we established enough concepts to look at a first statement and a proof of it.

Statement 3.2

17=1717 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 1717=17.

The claim holds by the definition of equality.

Though rather unexciting, this is our first real proof. Introducing the concept of equality would be rather pointless if we could not immediately determine whether any two objects are equal or not.

We can make things slightly more interesting by adding terms such as 1+21 + 21+2 to our language of statements. A term always denotes a way of obtaining objects of interest from other objects or other terms. In our examples, +++ denotes addition of two numbers.

Statement 3.3

5+12=175 + 12 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 175+12=17.

5+125 + 125+12 is 171717, and 17=1717 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 1717=17 by the definition of equality.

Introducing terms still does not add any real depth however: after evaluating the terms, their equality also either does or does not hold by definition. To formulate and prove more meaningful statements, we need the ability to define statements whose truth depends on the truth of other statements.

Quantifiers

The first such statements we examine are those of existential quantification, which is just fancy terminology for stating that at least one object satisfying a certain property exists:

Statement 3.4

There exists a squirrel sss such that s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex.

Define s≔Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \mathrm{Alex}s:=Alex, then s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex.

You prove statements of this form by presenting a witness, that is, an object that satisfies the required property. Note how two symbols that looks similar but have very different meaning appear in the proof: =\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}}= signifies a statement that is true if both sides of the symbol denote the same object, whereas ≔\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}}:= makes the name on the left side denote the object on the right side.

As existential quantification allows us to construct statements that contain other statements, we can formulate statements with multiple, nested levels of quantification:

Statement 3.5

There exists a squirrel sss such that there exists a squirrel ttt such that s=ts \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ts=t.

Define s≔Charlies \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \mathrm{Charlie}s:=Charlie and t≔Charliet \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \mathrm{Charlie}t:=Charlie, then s=ts \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ts=t.

Besides existential quantification for asserting that a statement holds for at least one object, there is universal quantification for asserting that a statement holds for all objects:

Statement 3.6

For all natural numbers nnn it holds that n=nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} nn=n.

Let nnn be a natural number. Then n=nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} nn=n by the definition of equality.

Because there are infinitely many natural numbers, we cannot prove a statement about every single number by going through all of them; we need our proofs to be of finite size. The only way to prove statements of universal quantification is by showing that the property in question holds for an arbitrarily chosen object, one about which we know nothing except that it is the kind of object that the statement speaks of. Throughout the book, we always use the phrase Let xxx be a something. to express when this is happening. Proving statements by working with arbitrarily chosen, featureless objects usually takes some getting used to, but it is the only way of proving statements about infinitely many objects.

The phrase Let xxx be a something. is also sometimes used as a different way of phrasing statements of universal quantification, most often to specify in advance which kinds of objects a statement will talk about. The following example expresses exactly the same fact as Statement  3.6 (and thus admits an identical proof), merely formulated differently:

Statement 3.7

Let nnn be a natural number.

Then n=nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} nn=n.

Let nnn be a natural number. Then n=nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} nn=n by the definition of equality.

Definitions

Existential quantification and universal quantification enable us to define a more interesting relation between numbers than equality:

Definition 3.8

Let nnn and mmm be natural numbers.

We say that nnn is less than or equal to mmm, written as n≤mn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} mn≤m, if there exists a natural number kkk such that n+k=mn + k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} mn+k=m.

With this concept defined, we can formulate statements about numbers being less than or equal to other numbers:

Statement 3.9

12≤1712 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 1712≤17.

Define k≔5k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} 5k:=5. Then 12+k=12+5=17,\begin{align*}12 + k &\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 12 + 5\\ &\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17,\end{align*}12+k​=12+5=17,​ and thus n≤17n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 17n≤17.

Statement 3.10

Let nnn be a natural number.

Then n≤nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} nn≤n.

Define k≔0k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} 0k:=0. Then n+k=n+0=n,\begin{align*}n + k &\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} n + 0\\ &\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} n,\end{align*}n+k​=n+0=n,​ and thus n≤nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} nn≤n.

As you can see, a definition is nothing but a shorthand; proofs proceed by unfolding the definition and then continuing as if the body of the definition had been used rather than its shorter name. Naming recurring concepts is vital to achieving concise yet precise communication, and it can also help in clarifying thought processes. This can lead to short sentences carrying large amounts of information, but this information can always be recovered by repeatedly unfolding definitions.

This book tries to make the unfolding of definitions as easy as possible: you can hover over any defined terminology (for example, less than or equal to) or symbol (for example, ≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤), and the definition will open in a tooltip. The terminology or symbol is also a hyperlink to its definition.

Exercise 3.2

Prove that for all natural numbers nnn it holds that 0≤n0 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} n0≤n.

Originally, equality was our only way of moving from objects of interest to statements of truth: 171717 is not a statement (it is neither true nor false), but 17=1817 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 1817=18 is (albeit a false one). The newly defined concept of numbers being less than or equal to each other achieves the same lift of conceptual level. Notice that its definition relies on equality again. Every such definition can ultimately be broken down to the level of equality comparisons.

Aside from introducing new ways of combining objects of interest into statements, we can also define new terms, that is, new ways of compactly describing objects of interest based on other objects:

Definition 3.11

Let nnn be a natural number.

We denote by twice⁡(n)\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/twice.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#twice}{\operatorname{twice}(}}n\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/twice.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#twice}{)}}twice(n) the number n+nn + nn+n.

Statement 3.12

There exists a natural number nnn such that twice⁡(n)=n\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/twice.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#twice}{\operatorname{twice}(}}n\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/twice.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#twice}{)}} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ntwice(n)=n.

Utilizing Quantifications

Once a statement of universal quantification has been proven, the acquired knowledge can be used in other proofs:

Statement 3.13

5≤55 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 55≤5.

555 is a natural number. Thus, by Statement  3.10, 5≤55 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 55≤5.

It would not have been difficult to prove Statement  3.13 directly, but utilizing Statement  3.10 saved us the trouble of finding a suitable number kkk to add to 555. In fact, our proof works even if we have no idea how ≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤ is defined. This hints at one of the key strengths of deductive reasoning: we can reason in terms of high-level concepts (≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤) while still getting the guarantee that everything works because it can ultimately be broken down to simple and correct building blocks (equality and existential quantification in this case).

Using a statement of existential quantification is less straightforward, because you merely know that some witness satisfying a certain property exists, but you do not know anything else about that witness. Thus you cannot utilize any further properties of the witness in your proof.

Statement 3.14

There exists a natural number nnn such that n≤18n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 18n≤18.

From Statement  3.9 we obtain nnn such that n≤17n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 17n≤17. From the definition of ≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤ we obtain kkk such that n+k=17n + k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17n+k=17.

If n+k=17n + k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17n+k=17, then also (n+k)+1=17+1\htmlClass{grouping_paren}{(}n + k\htmlClass{grouping_paren}{)} + 1 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17 + 1(n+k)+1=17+1, then also (n+k)+1=18\htmlClass{grouping_paren}{(}n + k\htmlClass{grouping_paren}{)} + 1 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 18(n+k)+1=18, and then also n+(k+1)=18n + \htmlClass{grouping_paren}{(}k + 1\htmlClass{grouping_paren}{)} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 18n+(k+1)=18.

k+1k + 1k+1 is a natural number, so we have found a number that we can add to nnn to obtain 181818. Or in other words, we have proven that n≤18n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 18n≤18.

Compared to directly choosing a suitable nnn, this proof is of course needlessly complicated. But it demonstrates how to work with this number nnn that we know very little about. There are 18 different numbers which are possible candidates for this nnn, each of them with unique properties not shared by any other number. Most of these properties are completely unrelated to the proof at hand.

If we worked with any specific number, we might be distracted by its properties. By working with the abstract value nnn that we obtained from Statement  3.9 however, only the relevant property of being less than or equal to 171717 is available to our reasoning, which protects us from losing ourselves in irrelevant details.

You can argue that knowing a statement of universal quantification is more useful than knowing a statement of existential quantification, because it can be more readily used in further proofs. This is offset by the fact that statements of existential quantification are much easier to prove — providing a specific witness is easier than working with abstract values which can represent any number of specific values.

Interestingly enough, you can also take on a completely opposite view. What is interesting about properties that hold for every single object there is? Existential quantification conveys the actually useful facts, it talks about the hidden gems among the masses of uninteresting objects. Unfortunately, these statements are much more difficult to prove — you need a clever idea for finding an object with the desired properties, rather than being handed an uncomplicated, distraction-free, abstract value to work with.

Names

At this point, we should briefly discuss names, which we have started using in terms such as n+k=17n + k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17n+k=17 without prior discussion. Names can stand in for objects or terms, but they never drop out of nowhere. Every name has to be introduced before it can appear in a proof: If we have no idea what xxx is, how are we to determine whether x=6x \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 6x=6 is true or false?

There is only a limited number of ways in which names can be introduced, and we have already seen all of them:

  • Formulating universal quantifications: For all squirrels sss it holds that….
  • Proving universal quantifications: Let sss be a squirrel, then….
  • Formulating existential quantifications: There exists a squirrel sss such that….
  • Proving existential quantifications: Obtain a squirrel sss such that….
  • Explicit naming: Define s≔…s \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \ldotss:=….

Each time you see a name, and each time you use one yourself, it must have been introduced by exactly one of these constructs. The precise wording for the construct can of course vary, as long as it is clear which of the five constructs is meant. But sticking to exactly one formulation per construct will make your proofs easier to follow.

Name introductions usually make the name available up until the end of the current text component, for example, until the end of the current statement, definition, (sub-)exercise, and so on. That is why the statements in this chapter quantify nnn again and again.

Quantifier Order

Does it matter in which order the quantifications of a statement occur? It does not for nested existential quantifications such as those of Statement  3.5: It makes no difference in which order we define the witnesses, so the same proofs still work if the order of the quantifications in the statement is swapped. The same is the case for nested statements of universal quantification, the order in which we obtain the featureless values for which to prove the desired properties does not matter for the proof to be correct.

These observations are examples of meta statements, statements about statements. We are not yet able to rigorously prove them, but, by the end of this book, we will do exactly that. For now, we merely state some particularly useful and simple meta statements and allow their (often implicit) use when writing proofs.

Even though we do not prove meta statements yet, we can be a bit more precise in formulating them. We say that two statements mean the same if, whenever we can prove one of them, we can also prove the other. Restating our observations about quantifier order: If we swap two successive existential quantifications (or two successive universal quantifications), the old and new statement mean the same.

When interleaving existential quantification and universal quantification however, the quantifier order can make a difference, as exemplified by the following statement:

Statement 3.15

For all natural numbers nnn there exists a natural number mmm such that n=mn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} mn=m.

Let nnn be a natural number. Define m≔nm \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} nm:=n. Then n=mn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} mn=m.

This statement is true, but what happens if we swap the order of the quantifiers?

Falsehood 3.16

There exists a natural number mmm such that for all natural numbers nnn we have n=mn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} mn=m.

???

Falsehood  3.16 is clearly different from Statement  3.15, there certainly is no number that is equal to every number. But how do we disprove a statement? For that, we need to examine the concept of negated statements.

Negations

The negation of a statement is a new statement that is true if the old statement is false, and vice versa. Or in other words, a negation asserts that another statement does not hold. How do we prove a negated statement? That depends on the kind of statement that is being negated. The easiest case is that of an equality, whose truth can be derived directly by definition:

Statement 3.17

It does not hold that 5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6.

5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6 does not hold by the definition of equality, so the negation of 5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6 does hold.

Another easy case is that of a negated negation (a double negation), which is simply canceled out.

Statement 3.18

It does not hold that it does not hold that 5=55 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 55=5.

The statement that remains after eliminating the double negation is 5=55 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 55=5, which holds by the definition of equality.

More interesting are negations of quantifications. If a statement does not hold for all objects, then there has to exist a counterexample. In other words, we can prove the negation of a statement of universal quantification by changing it to existential quantification and moving the negation past the quantifier:

Statement 3.19

It does not hold for all squirrels sss that s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex.

We can prove the statement by showing that there exists a squirrel sss such that it does not hold that s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex. Define s≔Blakes \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \mathrm{Blake}s:=Blake, then it does not hold that s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex.

Conversely, if no object satisfying a property exists, then all objects do not satisfy that property. That is, we can prove the negation of a statement of existential quantification by changing it to universal quantification and moving the negation past the quantifier.

Statement 3.20

It does not hold that there exists a squirrel sss such that it does not hold that s=ss \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ss=s.

Disproving statements by proving their negation makes sense only if we assume that every statement must either be true or false. This assumption is known as the principle of bivalence, and while it might feel self-evident, it is possible to find self-consistent frameworks for deductive reasoning that reject it. To keep things compact, this book consistently sticks to the prevalent school of classical mathematics that accepts the principle of bivalence.

The proof of Statement  3.21 is representative for how we usually prove negated statements: First, we convert the statement into an equivalent one in which the only negated substatements are statements of equality, and then, we prove that new statement as usual, without having to worry about negations anymore.

Because this conversion is completely mechanical, it is often left implicit in written proofs. Incorrect manipulation of negations is a common source of errors for new learners however, so it is a good habit to carefully and explicitly think through the process of threading a negation through a statement, whenever you encounter it.

Conjunctions and Disjunctions

Between existential quantification, universal quantification, and negation, we have covered the most complicated kinds of statements. What remains are a few statements which relate truth of other statements to each other.

A conjunction asserts that two substatements are both true:

Utilizing Conjunctions and Disjunctions

Once a conjunction is proven, both of its substatements can be assumed to be true:

Statement 3.26

Alex=Alex\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}Alex=Alex.

Alex=Alex\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}Alex=Alex by Statement  3.22.

Utilizing a disjunction to prove another statement is more difficult however, because either of its substatements might not be true. So it does not suffice to assume any one of the substatements and finish the proof from there. Instead, you need to demonstrate that the proof can be completed regardless of which substatement is assumed to be true. This is called a case distinction and results in having to complete two independent proofs.

Negating Conjunctions and Disjunctions

The similarities to the behavior of quantifiers continues with the way that you prove the negations of conjunctions and disjunctions.

If two statements are not both true, then at least one of them has to be false. In other words, you can prove a negated conjunction by changing it to a disjunction and negating its substatements.

Statement 3.28

It does not hold that Alex=Alex\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}Alex=Alex and Alex=Blake\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Blake}Alex=Blake.

The latter holds by the definition of equality.

Conversely, if two statements are not both false, then at least one of them has to be true. In other words, we can prove a negated disjunction by changing it to a conjunction and negating its substatements.

Statement 3.29

It does not hold that Alex=Blake\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Blake}Alex=Blake or Alex=Charlie\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Charlie}Alex=Charlie.

Meta Statements for Conjunctions And Disjunctions

We can observe some simple meta statements for conjunctions and disjunctions. The order of the two substatements of a conjunction or disjunction does not matter: bla and blubb and blubb and bla mean the same, as do bla or blubb and blubb or bla. These laws are called the commutativity of conjunctions and the commutativity of disjunctions respectively.

Because a conjunction has exactly two substatements, we technically cannot state directly that three different statements all must hold, we instead either have to say that bla and (blubb and blip) or that (bla and blubb) and blip. Both ways of grouping the conjunctions mean the same, which is why we do not require making the grouping explicit. This law is called the associativity of conjunctions, and allows us to think in terms of conjunctions with arbitrarily many substatements.

For disjunctions we have the analogous associativity of disjunctions, and we can accordingly think in terms of disjunctions with arbitrarily many substatements. We even allow case distinctions with more than two cases, as long as the disjunction over all cases is known to be true.

Implications

Deductive reasoning is about deriving statements from other statements. Whether some statement — the conclusion — can be derived from some other statement — the premise — is in itself either true or false, that is, it is a statement itself. Such a statement is called an implication.

Utilizing Implications

Once an implication is proven, you can use it to show that its conclusion holds by proving its premise:

Statement 3.32

3≤73 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 73≤7

3≤53 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 53≤5 because 3+2=53 + 2 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 53+2=5. With Statement  3.30 follows that 3≤73 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 73≤7.

Now consider a situation where bla implies blubb, and blubb implies blip. Then we also know that bla implies blip, because if we can assume bla, then the first implication gives us blubb, from which the second implication gives us blip. This property of implications is called the transitivity of implications, and is crucial to deductive reasoning: the transitivity of implications is the reason why we can prove statements by chaining multiple small steps where each step implies the next one.

Negating Implications

An implication expresses that its conclusion cannot be false while its premise is a true. Consequently you disprove an implication, or equivalently, you prove a negated implication, by showing that its premise holds but its conclusion does not.

From a similar line of reasoning we can derive another meta statement. If the conclusion of an implication does not hold, then the premise of the implication cannot hold either. This reformulation of an implication is called its contrapositive: the contrapositive of bla implies blubb is not blubb implies not bla. An implication and its contrapositive mean the same, this is called the law of contraposition.

Statement 3.34

Let nnn be a natural number.

If nnn is not less than or equal to 777, then nnn is not less than or equal to 555.

The statement is the contrapositive of Statement  3.30 and thus holds by the law of contraposition.

Absurd Implications

Any time you prove an implication, you start by assuming its premise to be true. So what happens if the premise is a false statement? Perhaps surprisingly, the implication as a whole turns out to be true in that case. We demonstrate this with an example:

Statement 3.35

If 0=10 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 10=1, then Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is currently eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon.

Assume 0=10 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 10=1. Further notice that 0≠10 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/neq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#neq}{\neq}} 10=1 holds by the definition of equality. Thus also the disjunction 0≠10 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/neq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#neq}{\neq}} 10=1 or Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is currently eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon holds.

Because 0=10 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 10=1, the first substatement of that disjunction cannot be true, so the second substatement has to be. Thus, we have shown that Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is currently eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon.

Eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon is rather atypical behavior for squirrels, and yet we were able to prove it by assuming a falsehood. In fact, we could have proven any statement, including even that Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is not currently eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon. The fact that every statement can be proven from a falsehood is vivaciously called the principle of explosion.

When proving an implication with a false premise, we get to assume a falsehood to hold. By the principle of explosion, we can then prove any statement. In particular, we can prove the conclusion of the implication, and if we can prove the conclusion by assuming the premise, then the implication as a whole holds.

This can lead to statements to whose truth it might take some getting used to, such as the one in the following exercise:

Exercise 3.18

Prove that there exist a squirrel sss such that s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex implies s=Blakes \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Blake}s=Blake.

Proofs by Contradiction

Implications with a false premise always hold, but what about implications with a false conclusion? If the implication is to hold, we must be able to prove its conclusion, which should be impossible if the conclusion is false. Except, of course, if the premise is false as well. So we can observe the following: If an implication with a false conclusion holds, its premise must be false.

We can use this as a proof technique: If we can manage to prove a falsehood by assuming the negation of some statement, then the negation of that statement must have been false, and thus, the original statement must have been true. Such a proof is called a proof by contradiction, contradiction being synonymous to falsehood in this context.

Statement 3.36

There is no squirrel sss such that for all squirrels ttt holds s=ts \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ts=t.

Suppose toward a contradiction that there exists a squirrel sss such that for all squirrels ttt holds s=ts \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} ts=t.

Then we know that both s=Alexs \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}s=Alex and s=Blakes \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Blake}s=Blake, and thus Alex=Blake\mathrm{Alex} \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Blake}Alex=Blake. This is a contradiction, the assumption must have been false, and thus, the original statement must be true.

Bi-implications

When two statements imply each other, then either both of them or none of them have to hold. This is called a bi-implication.
Statement 3.37

5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6 if and only if 6=76 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 76=7

  ⟹  \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/implication.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#implication}{\implies}}⟹:

Assume 5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6. Then, by the principle of explosion, 6=76 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 76=7.

  ⟸  \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/implication.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#implication}{\impliedby}}⟸:

Assume 6=76 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 76=7. Then, by the principle of explosion, 5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6.

You prove a bi-implication by proving both implications separately. You prove a negated bi-implication by disproving either of the implications.

A common variation on proving both implications directly is to prove the contrapositive of one of them:

There are a few different ways of formulating bi-implications:

  • bla if and only if blubb
  • bla iff blubb
  • bla exactly if blubb
  • bla is equivalent to blubb
  • bla and blubb imply each other
  • bla   ⟺  \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/bi_implication.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#bi_implication}{\iff}}⟺ blubb

Finally, the concept of statements that mean the same is exactly that of statements that imply each other.
