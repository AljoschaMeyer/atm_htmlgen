Chapter 1
Deductive Reasoning

Mathematicians go about understanding the world in a very rigorous manner; they do not guess or believe things, but they prove them. Once a fact has been proven, it can be used for proving other facts. In this chapter, we take a close look at how to operate in this framework. As a human being, you are already adept at reasoning about the world, so some of what follows will likely be familiar to you already. Turning your intuitive understanding into explicit knowledge will improve your ability to reason this way.

1.1: Gathering Knowledge

Deductive reasoning is the process of concluding facts from assumptions. If we assume that squirrels are always hungry, and that Alex is a squirrel, we can conclude that Alex is always hungry. A framework for deductive reasoning must provide clear rules on which deduction steps are valid. If, for example, we knew that Vic was always hungry, we should not be able to conclude that Vic is a squirrel; Vic could be a badger for all we know.

Contrast this with inductive reasoning, the process of hypothesizing causes from observed facts. From observing that the sun rises and sets every day, you might form the potential explanation that the sun orbits the earth. Later observations might invalidate a hypothesis, but a hypothesis can never be proven beyond doubt.

Even though inductive reasoning can lead to false conclusions, it has its firm place in humanity's quest for knowledge. Physics or chemistry, for example, would make little sense if they were not grounded in observations of the world around us. They can strive to find increasingly accurate models of the world, but there can always be a new observation that contradicts the existing models.

Unlike the natural sciences, mathematics relies on deductive reasoning alone. Rigorous adherence to the principle that every claim has to follow from either assumptions or previously proven claims may not be a normal mode of operation for your brain, but learning to operate that way comes with a number of benefits:

  • Proven knowledge is permanent. It does not degrade, it does not go out of style, it will not be falsified by a future observation. This has enabled mathematicians to build up knowledge over centuries.
  • Proven knowledge is verifiable. It does not matter who wrote a proof, whether you trust them or not. If every single step follows the deduction rules, then the resulting information is true.
  • Proven knowledge is inspectable. If you do not understand why something is true, the proof breaks it down for you into small, almost obviously correct steps. More often than not, the person who wrote that initially incomprehensible proof is yourself from the past. But if the proof is well written, you are always able to untangle it.
  • Proven knowledge is composable. Once a fact has been proven, it can be reused in further proofs. Furthermore, the details of how it was proven do not affect that reuse in any way.

Once mastered, deductive reasoning enables you to think thoughts you would not have been able to think otherwise, because you would have been overwhelmed by the sheer amount of details and the accumulation of tiny errors.

A key property of deductive reasoning is that the mere form of a statement already implies how it can be proven. Suppose we want to prove that there is at least one hungry badger, how do we go about it? By presenting an actual hungry badger. How do you prove that there is at least one flying squirrel? By presenting an actual flying squirrel. If however we wanted to prove that all squirrels can fly, presenting an individual flying one would not help at all.

Consequently, the fewer different forms of statements there are, the less complicated the system of deductive reasoning becomes. In the next section, we examine a small number of constructs for forming statements, chosen so that any statement of interest can be expressed using these constructs. The accompanying examples may feel trivial or even boring in their simplicity. That is an incredibly important and desired aspect of the system. Proving facts should not in itself be difficult; all complexity should come from the subject matter at hand, not the proof mechanism itself. Humanity has expended a great deal of collective brainpower so that the contents of the following section would become as simple as possible.

Quantifiers

The first such statements we examine are those of existential quantification, which is just fancy terminology for stating that at least one object satisfying a certain property exists:

Statement 1.4

There exists a squirrel sss such that s=Alex.s \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}\text{.}s=Alex.

Proof of Statement  1.4

Define s≔Alex,s \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \mathrm{Alex}\text{,}s:=Alex, then s=Alex.s \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} \mathrm{Alex}\text{.}s=Alex.

You prove statements of this form by presenting a witness, that is, an object that satisfies the required property. Note how two symbols that looks similar but have very different meaning appear in the proof: =\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}}= signifies a statement that is true if both sides of the symbol denote the same object, whereas ≔\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}}:= makes the name on the left side denote the object on the right side.

Presenting a single witness proves the statement. Whether there are multiple other witnesses does not change that.

Besides existential quantification for asserting that a statement holds for at least one object, there is universal quantification for asserting that a statement holds for all objects:

Statement 1.6

For all natural numbers nnn it holds that n=n.n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} n\text{.}n=n.

Proof of Statement  1.6

Let nnn be a natural number. Then n=nn \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} nn=n by the definition of equality.

Because there are infinitely many natural numbers, we cannot prove a statement about every single number by going through all of them; we need our proofs to be of finite size. The only way to prove statements of universal quantification is by showing that the property in question holds for an arbitrarily chosen object, one about which we know nothing except that it is the kind of object that the statement speaks of. Throughout the book, we always use the phrase Let xxx be a something. to express when this is happening. Proving statements by working with arbitrarily chosen, featureless objects usually takes some getting used to, but it is the only way of proving statements about infinitely many objects.

The phrase Let xxx be a something. is also sometimes used as a different way of phrasing statements of universal quantification, most often to specify in advance which kinds of objects a statement will talk about. The following example expresses exactly the same fact as Statement  1.6 (and thus admits an identical proof), merely formulated differently:

Let nnn be a natural number.

Then n=n.n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} n\text{.}n=n.

Existential quantification and universal quantification enable us to define a more interesting relation between numbers than equality:

Naming recurring concepts is vital to achieving concise yet precise communication, and it can also help in clarifying thought processes. This can lead to short sentences carrying large amounts of information, but this information can always be recovered by repeatedly unfolding definitions.

Utilizing Quantifications

Once a statement of universal quantification has been proven, the acquired knowledge can be used in other proofs:

Statement 1.14

Claim: 5≤5.5 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 5\text{.}5≤5.

Proof of Statement  1.14

555 is a natural number. Thus, by Statement  1.10, 5≤5.5 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 5\text{.}5≤5.

It would not have been difficult to prove Statement  1.14 directly, but utilizing Statement  1.10 saved us the trouble of finding a suitable number kkk to add to 5.5\text{.}5. In fact, our proof works even if we have no idea how ≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤ is defined. This hints at one of the key strengths of deductive reasoning: we can reason in terms of high-level concepts (≤\htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}}≤) while still getting the guarantee that everything works because it can ultimately be broken down to simple and correct building blocks (equality and existential quantification in this case).

Using a statement of existential quantification is less straightforward, because you merely know that some witness satisfying a certain property exists, but you do not know anything else about that witness. Thus you cannot utilize any further properties of the witness in your proof.

There exists a natural number nnn such that n≤18.n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/leq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#leq}{\leq}} 18\text{.}n≤18.

Compared to directly choosing a suitable n,n\text{,}n, this proof is of course needlessly complicated. But it demonstrates how to work with this number nnn that we know very little about. There are 18 different numbers which are possible candidates for this n,n\text{,}n, each of them with unique properties not shared by any other number. Most of these properties are completely unrelated to the proof at hand.

If we worked with any specific number, we might be distracted by its properties. By working with the abstract value nnn that we obtained from Statement  1.9 however, only the relevant property of being less than or equal to 171717 is available to our reasoning, which protects us from losing ourselves in irrelevant details.

You can argue that knowing a statement of universal quantification is more useful than knowing a statement of existential quantification, because it can be more readily used in further proofs. This is offset by the fact that statements of existential quantification are much easier to prove — providing a specific witness is easier than working with abstract values which can represent any number of specific values.

Interestingly enough, you can also take on a completely opposite view. What is interesting about properties that hold for every single object there is? Existential quantification conveys the actually useful facts, it talks about the hidden gems among the masses of uninteresting objects. Unfortunately, these statements are much more difficult to prove — you need a clever idea for finding an object with the desired properties, rather than being handed an uncomplicated, distraction-free, abstract value to work with.

Names

At this point, we should briefly discuss names, which we have started using in terms such as n+k=17n + k \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 17n+k=17 without prior discussion. Names can stand in for objects or terms, but they never drop out of nowhere. Every name has to be introduced before it can appear in a proof: If we have no idea what xxx is, how are we to determine whether x=6x \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 6x=6 is true or false?

There is only a limited number of ways in which names can be introduced, and we have already seen all of them:

  • Formulating universal quantifications: For all squirrels sss it holds that….
  • Proving universal quantifications: Let sss be a squirrel, then….
  • Formulating existential quantifications: There exists a squirrel sss such that….
  • Proving existential quantifications: Obtain a squirrel sss such that….
  • Explicit naming: Define s≔…s \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/defeq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#defeq}{\coloneqq}} \ldotss:=….

Each time you see a name, and each time you use one yourself, it must have been introduced by exactly one of these constructs. The precise wording for the construct can of course vary, as long as it is clear which of the five constructs is meant. But sticking to exactly one formulation per construct will make your proofs easier to follow.

Name introductions usually make the name available up until the end of the current text component, for example, until the end of the current statement, definition, (sub-)exercise, and so on. That is why the statements in this chapter quantify nnn again and again.

Quantifier Order

Does it matter in which order the quantifications of a statement occur? It does not for nested existential quantifications such as those of Statement  1.5: It makes no difference in which order we define the witnesses, so the same proofs still work if the order of the quantifications in the statement is swapped. The same is the case for nested statements of universal quantification, the order in which we obtain the featureless values for which to prove the desired properties does not matter for the proof to be correct.

These observations are examples of meta statements, statements about statements. We are not yet able to rigorously prove them, but, by the end of this book, we will do exactly that. For now, we merely state some particularly useful and simple meta statements and allow their (often implicit) use when writing proofs.

Even though we do not prove meta statements yet, we can be a bit more precise in formulating them. We say that two statements mean the same if, whenever we can prove one of them, we can also prove the other. Restating our observations about quantifier order: If we swap two successive existential quantifications (or two successive universal quantifications), the old and new statement mean the same.

When interleaving existential quantification and universal quantification however, the quantifier order can make a difference, as exemplified by the following statement:

For all natural numbers nnn there exists a natural number mmm such that n=m.n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} m\text{.}n=m.

Then n=m.n \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} m\text{.}n=m.

Falsehood  1.17 is clearly different from Statement  1.16, there certainly is no number that is equal to every number. But how do we disprove a statement? For that, we need to examine the concept of negated statements.

Negations

The negation of a statement is a new statement that is true if the old statement is false, and vice versa. Or in other words, a negation asserts that another statement does not hold. How do we prove a negated statement? That depends on the kind of statement that is being negated. The easiest case is that of an equality, whose truth can be derived directly by definition:

Statement 1.18

It does not hold that 5=6.5 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 6\text{.}5=6.

Proof of Statement  1.18

5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6 does not hold by the definition of equality, so the negation of 5=65 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 65=6 does hold.

Disproving statements by proving their negation makes sense only if we assume that every statement must either be true or false. This assumption is known as the principle of bivalence, and while it might feel self-evident, it is possible to find self-consistent frameworks for deductive reasoning that reject it. The proof of Statement  1.22 is representative for how we usually prove negated statements: First, we convert the statement into an equivalent one in which the only negated substatements are statements of equality, and then, we prove that new statement as usual, without having to worry about negations anymore.

Because this conversion is completely mechanical, it is often left implicit in written proofs. Incorrect manipulation of negations is a common source of errors for new learners however, so it is a good habit to carefully and explicitly think through the process of threading a negation through a statement, whenever you encounter it.

Meta Statements for Conjunctions And Disjunctions

We can observe some simple meta statements for conjunctions and disjunctions. The order of the two substatements of a conjunction or disjunction does not matter: bla and blubb and blubb and bla mean the same, as do bla or blubb and blubb or bla. These laws are called the commutativity of conjunctions and the commutativity of disjunctions respectively.

Because a conjunction has exactly two substatements, we technically cannot state directly that three different statements all must hold, we instead either have to say that bla and (blubb and blip) or that (bla and blubb) and blip. Both ways of grouping the conjunctions mean the same, which is why we do not require making the grouping explicit. This law is called the associativity of conjunctions, and allows us to think in terms of conjunctions with arbitrarily many substatements.

For disjunctions we have the analogous associativity of disjunctions, and we can accordingly think in terms of disjunctions with arbitrarily many substatements. We even allow case distinctions with more than two cases, as long as the disjunction over all cases is known to be true.

Exercise 1.11

Find and summarize as many of the analogies between the properties of conjunction/disjunction and universal/existential quantification as you can.

  • Universal quantifications require something of all objects, and conjunctions of all substatements. Existential quantifications require something of at least one object, and disjunctions of at least one substatement.
  • Changing a true universal quantification into an existential quantification preserves truth, as does changing a true conjunction into a disjunction. Changing a false existential quantification into a universal quantification preserves falsehood, as does changing a false disjunction into a conjunction.
  • Negation is propagated past a quantifier by swapping the quantifier and negating the substatement. Negation is propagated past a conjunction or disjunction by swapping to the other and negating the substatements.

  • Proving universal quantifications and conjunctions requires more powerful arguments than proving existential quantifications and disjunctions respectively, because the prior talk about multiple substatements respectively objects at a time, whereas the latter only talk about individual ones.

    Proving existential quantifications and disjunctions requires more intelligent choice however, because you need to choose a suitable object respectively substatement multiple options.

  • Using existential quantifications and disjunctions requires more powerful follow-up arguments than using universal quantifications and conjunctions: existential quantifications give you a mostly featureless object whereas universal quantifications give you additional information about an arbitrary object, and disjunctions require you to solve multiple cases whereas conjunctions simply progress the proof.

    Using universal quantifications and conjunctions requires more intelligent choice however: you need to choose which object respectively substatement to gain more information about.

  • The grouping amongst multiple, successive universal quantifications does not matter, neither does that of multiple, successive existential quantifications. The same story holds for conjunctions and disjunctions. But when interleaving universal quantifications and existential quantifications, or when interleaving conjunctions and disjunctions, different groupings can lead to statements that do not mean the same.

An implication expresses that its conclusion cannot be false while its premise is a true. Consequently you disprove an implication, or equivalently, you prove a negated implication, by showing that its premise holds but its conclusion does not.

Absurd Implications

Any time you prove an implication, you start by assuming its premise to be true. So what happens if the premise is a false statement? Perhaps surprisingly, the implication as a whole turns out to be true in that case. We demonstrate this with an example:

Statement 1.36

If 0=1,0 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 1\text{,}0=1, then Alex\mathrm{Alex}Alex is currently eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon.

Assume 0=1.0 \htmlData{preview=http://localhost:1234/previews/eq.html}{\href{http://localhost:1234/deductive_reasoning.html#eq}{=}} 1\text{.}0=1. Eating pumpkin pie in a hot air balloon is rather atypical behavior for squirrels, and yet we were able to prove it by assuming a falsehood. When proving an implication with a false premise, we get to assume a falsehood to hold. By the principle of explosion, we can then prove any statement. In particular, we can prove the conclusion of the implication, and if we can prove the conclusion by assuming the premise, then the implication as a whole holds.

Proofs by Contradiction

Implications with a false premise always hold, but what about implications with a false conclusion? If the implication is to hold, we must be able to prove its conclusion, which should be impossible if the conclusion is false. Except, of course, if the premise is false as well. So we can observe the following: If an implication with a false conclusion holds, its premise must be false.

We can use this as a proof technique: If we can manage to prove a falsehood by assuming the negation of some statement, then the negation of that statement must have been false, and thus, the original statement must have been true. Such a proof is called a proof by contradiction, contradiction being synonymous to falsehood in this context.

You prove a bi-implication by proving both implications separately. You prove a negated bi-implication by disproving either of the implications.

There are a few different ways of formulating bi-implications:

  • bla if and only if blubb
  • bla iff blubb
  • bla exactly if blubb

Finally, the concept of statements that mean the same is exactly that of statements that imply each other.

Words of Caution

Deductive reasoning is a powerful tool. Knowing that some conclusions you reached are provably correct makes it easy to dismiss anyone who disagrees with you. But always remember that every proof ultimately rests on certain assumptions about the objects of interest.

While you might know for certain that a conclusion follows from certain assumptions, you cannot know whether those assumptions were correct in the first place. Every proof is only as good as its assumptions; deductive reasoning offers guaranteed self-consistency, not absolute truths. If you start with awful assumptions, you can arrive at awful conclusions. Reaching those conclusions in a provably correct manner does not justify them at all, it is your assumptions you need to scrutinize.

In mathematics, almost everyone agrees on the same assumptions, and the few points of contention are well-known (key phrases are intuitionistic logic and the axiom of choice). But the world we inhabit is complex, and human beings even more so, making it difficult or even impossible for multiple people to work from identical assumptions on real-world problems.

1.4: Exercises

Every chapter of this book ends with a collection of exercises that recapitulate the contents of the chapter and have you develop related concepts on your own. Every chapter except this one that is, as you will practice your deductive reasoning skills throughout the whole book.

Like every new skill, you become better at mathematics by practicing. You do not solve exercises to check whether you understood the material, you solve the exercises so that you do understand the material. You will spend more time on thinking about exercises than on reading the book.

To get the most out of these exercises, try to follow some principles:

  • You do not need to solve every single exercise. If an exercise bores you, you are probably not learning anything and you can safely skip it.
  • Always prove that your solutions are correct. If an exercise asks you to give an object that satisfies certain properties, prove that it does indeed satisfy these properties.
  • Write down some of your solutions, you will miss crucial details if you do not. Giving written solutions to only some of the exercises will teach you more than solving all of them in your head. If you want to try digital typesetting, the most commonly used software is LaTeX. Writing by hand tends to be faster though.
  • Find other learners and talk about exercises that give you trouble. Exchange your write-ups and help each other improve them. Consider writing down solutions together.
  • If at all possible, gather feedback on your written solutions from a more experienced mathematician. Ask them to be thorough and scrutinize your work mercilessly, in content and formal rigor as well as in style and presentation. You are not only learning new concepts, you are also getting to know a new mode of communication.

When writing down solutions, try to adhere to the following guidelines:

  • Help your reader understand you. If the exercise asks you to either prove or disprove a statement, begin by telling the reader which of the two you will do. The reader should never need to backtrack in order to understand your solution. Omit all information that is not directly relevant to your solution.
  • Your reader has exactly the background knowledge given in the book up to the current point. You need to convince your reader of the correctness of your solutions. Your reader does not accept your intuitive reasoning, only knowledge derived from definitions counts. Write down computations step-by-step, annotating each step with the definition or proven statement that justifies it.
  • Introducing formal arguments with an intuitive explanation can sometimes increase legibility. If you choose to do so, clearly communicate which parts of your solution constitute guiding intuition and which parts constitute formal reasoning. No intuitive explanation should be without a formal counterpart.

Finally some hints for approaching difficult exercises:

  • You cannot solve an exercise you do not fully understand. Whenever you encounter a term in an exercise that you are unsure about, look up its definition. Never start an exercise if you cannot precisely define the task at hand.
  • Examining concrete examples often leads to ideas that are applicable in general. Rather than diving directly into a proof, first get a feeling for the behavior of specific objects. Or perhaps look for counterexamples and analyze why you just cannot find any.
  • Do not be discouraged if you struggle to solve an exercise. Always remember that you do not do exercises to measure your skills, but to hone them. The exercises you can easily solve are far less valuable than those you are having a hard time with. Keep in mind that moving on to another exercise and returning later to the difficult one can sometimes do wonders.
